Plain-Language Summary — What Happened

Marlore Anwandter wrote the songs that every Colombian child has grown up with since 1975. In 2025, she was paid $14,086 for the entire year.

Canticuentos is, by all accounts, the best-selling album in Colombian history. The official YouTube channel has 4.85 billion views and 5.98 million subscribers. On Spotify, Canticuentos has 736,000 monthly listeners. In 2024, ten of Marlore's songs were re-recorded by some of Colombia's biggest artists — including Juanes, Carlos Vives, Andrea Echeverri, and Andres Cepeda — and released as "Los Nuevos Canticuentos." That album won the 2025 Latin Grammy for Best Children's Music Album.

Despite all of this, the royalty statements show serious problems:

  • The Latin Grammy-winning album generated zero songwriter royalties. "Los Nuevos Canticuentos" re-records Marlore's original compositions. She should be paid every time those new recordings are streamed, downloaded, or performed. Not a single penny from this album appears anywhere in the 2025 statements.
  • Colombia's music royalty collection society (SAYCO) is completely absent. Whenever a song plays on Colombian radio, television, or in a restaurant or store, SAYCO is legally required to collect royalties for the songwriter. For the most popular children's songs in the country, there should be substantial income here. There is none.
  • Colombia streaming income collapsed by 99.96%. In the first quarter of 2025, the statements reported over 715,000 YouTube views from Colombia. By the fourth quarter, that number had dropped to 268. Spotify Colombia streams went from 17,337 to literally zero. Meanwhile, US streams stayed consistent — proving the songs weren't taken down. Something changed in how Colombia income is being tracked and reported.
  • The primary Colombia sub-publisher vanished. SONY/ATV Colombia — the entity that was collecting royalties in Colombia — reported 79 line items in Q1 and then completely disappeared from Q2 through Q4 with no explanation.
  • People have been added as co-writers on Marlore's songs without clear authorization. At least two songs have alternate-title registrations with additional "co-writers" (Luis Gomez Ramirez, Mario Hernandez, and an unnamed "Unknown Writer"). This dilutes Marlore's share of royalties on those songs.
  • Royalties are being reported years late. Some payments in the 2025 statements are for activity from 2018, 2020, and even 2010 — up to 15 years late. Hundreds of these old entries appear in blocks, which is consistent with withheld royalties being slowly released rather than legitimate processing delays.
  • Every song gets the exact same amount from Colombia's collecting society. Whether it's the most famous song in the catalog or the most obscure, ACODEM paid exactly $150.42 for mechanical royalties and $156.20 for sync royalties. This flat-rate approach ignores actual popularity data and shortchanges the biggest songs.

But there's an even bigger issue. Marlore is not just a songwriter — she is the author. She wrote the stories, the lyrics, and created the characters. Under Colombian law, this gives her far broader rights than just songwriter royalties. Her authored characters (Sammy El Heladero, La Bruja Loca, El Pirata Feroz, and dozens more) are being used in animated YouTube videos, an illustrated book, merchandise, and educational materials — all without any apparent compensation to her as the creator. The "Los Nuevos Canticuentos" was even published as a book (by Planeta Junior, one of the world's largest publishers) using her authored texts — and zero book royalties appear in any statement. Under Colombian copyright law, the original 1975 recording contract almost certainly cannot cover modern uses like streaming, animation, book publication, or digital distribution, because none of these existed when the contract was signed. If the contract didn't specify a duration, it may have expired in 1980.

The bottom line: A conservative estimate for songwriter royalties alone suggests $77,600 to $307,600 or more per year in missing income. But when author rights are factored in — book royalties, character licensing, animation rights, educational licensing, adaptation rights, and the potential invalidity of the 1975 contract — the total exposure for Codiscos and Spirit Music could be in the millions, especially with a historical lookback. The $14,086 Marlore received for the entire year of 2025 is a tiny fraction of what this catalog should generate.

Category A — Critical: Complete Absence of Expected Income Streams

  1. "Los Nuevos Canticuentos" — Zero songwriter mechanical royalties. Album released 2024 by Codiscos/Gaira Musica Local (Carlos Vives), featuring re-recordings of 10 Anwandter compositions by major-label artists (Juanes, Carlos Vives, Andrea Echeverri, Andres Cepeda, Felipe Pelaez, Pipe Bueno, Luisa Fernanda W, et al.).   2. SAYCO performance royalties — Completely absent. SAYCO (Sociedad de Autores y Compositores de Colombia) is the sole authorized collecting society for songwriters in Colombia under Andean Decision 351. Public performance royalties from radio, television, commercial venues, educational institutions, and digital platforms should flow through SAYCO. For a catalog of this cultural prominence, the complete absence of SAYCO income in all four 2025 statements is prima facie evidence of either (a) failure to register the catalog with SAYCO, (b) diversion of SAYCO collections to a different payee, or (c) SAYCO's own collection failures (noting SAYCO was sanctioned COP 5.3 billion in November 2025 for monopoly abuse and discriminatory fee practices). Independent inquiry to SAYCO is warranted.
  3. "Canticuentos Filarmonico" (2026) — Zero songwriter royalties. Orchestral re-recordings of 16 Anwandter compositions with the Orquesta Filarmonica de Medellin. Released on streaming platforms. Same songwriter mechanical obligation applies.

Category D — High: Delayed Reporting, Clawbacks, and Data Integrity

  1. Systematic delayed reporting consistent with drip-fed withholding. 2025 statements contain entries from as far back as 2010 (15-year lag). Large blocks of old royalties arrive in waves: 2018-2021 entries in Q2, 2022 entries in Q3, 2021-2022 entries in Q4. This pattern — historical royalties released in curated batches across different quarters — is consistent with deliberate withholding and strategic release rather than organic processing delays.
  2. Escalating negative adjustments (clawbacks). Q1: 0 negatives. Q2: 22 (-$0.23). Q3: 96 (-$3.28). Q4: 609 (-$35.70). The most popular songs (Ronda de las Vocales, Sammy El Heladero) receive the most clawbacks. Many reference 2021-2023 periods. While individually small, the acceleration pattern (0 to 609 entries in four quarters) and the targeting of top-earning songs warrants scrutiny.
  3. Future-dated periods. Q4 contains 203+ entries with period dates in 2026 and 2027. SAMMY EL HELADERO shows $177.06 received / $88.53 due for periods 01/2026-10/2026. Income for future periods that has not yet been earned cannot appear on a 2025 royalty statement. These entries require explanation — whether advance payments, data errors, or fabricated line items.
  4. ACODEM flat-rate distributions. Q1 ACODEM distributions are identical across all 10 reported songs ($150.42 mechanical, $156.20 sync) regardless of actual usage. For a catalog where the top song (Ronda de las Vocales, 100M+ YouTube views) vastly outperforms others, a usage-blind flat allocation materially disadvantages high-performing works. Estimated shortfall for top songs: $600-$900 each per distribution.

Applicable Law

  • Colombian Copyright Law (Ley 23 de 1982): Economic rights last life + 80 years. Moral rights are perpetual and inalienable. Transfers of economic rights are limited to the specific modalities, territory, and duration stated in the contract — vague or overbroad assignments may be challengeable.
  • Andean Decision 351 (1993): Exclusivity rule prohibits simultaneous membership in multiple collecting societies. This may create collection gaps if SAYCO's reciprocal agreements are inadequate.
  • Colombia Withholding Tax: 20-25% on royalties to non-residents (Decreto 1474). Payments to tax haven beneficiaries face 35%. Statements should be verified for correct WHT application.
  • SAYCO Sanction (Nov 2025): SIC imposed COP 5.3 billion fine for monopoly abuse — charging extra 10% admin fees to "administered holders" and imposing restrictive clauses preventing rights reservation. Repeat offense (previously sanctioned 2016). This institutional dysfunction may partially explain collection failures.

Critical Gap — Expected Income Not Found

⚠ SAYCO / ACINPRO Performance Royalties — Completely Absent

SAYCO (Sociedad de Autores y Compositores de Colombia) is the mandatory collective management organization for musical authors and composers in Colombia. For any song performed publicly, broadcast on radio or TV, or played in commercial venues in Colombia, SAYCO is legally required to collect and distribute performance royalties. ACINPRO covers performers and producers.

Canticuentos songs have been broadcast on Colombian children's television, radio, and educational platforms since 1975. For a catalog of this size and cultural prominence to generate zero SAYCO income in an entire calendar year is exceptional — and demands immediate clarification.

Possibilities include: (1) SAYCO income is being collected but retained at the publisher level and not passed through; (2) SAYCO has not been correctly registered for this catalog; (3) Income is being reported under a different payee name; (4) The statements provided do not represent the complete picture of income streams. Additionally, SAYCO's own compliance issues (fined COP 5.3 billion in Nov 2025) may be a contributing factor requiring independent investigation.

Colombia imposes a 20–25% withholding tax on royalties to non-residents. These statements should also be checked for correct WHT application on all Colombia-territory payments.
